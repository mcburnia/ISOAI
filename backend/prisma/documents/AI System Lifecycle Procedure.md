# AI System Lifecycle Procedure

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the governance procedure used by the organisation to manage the lifecycle of artificial intelligence systems.

Artificial intelligence systems must be introduced, operated, and retired in a controlled manner. This ensures that risks are considered before deployment and that systems remain appropriate for their intended purpose.

The lifecycle procedure ensures that AI systems are subject to appropriate review and oversight throughout their operational life.

This procedure forms part of the Artificial Intelligence Management System established in alignment with ISO/IEC 42001.

---

## 2. Scope

This procedure applies to all artificial intelligence systems within the scope of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

This includes systems that are:

- developed internally
- deployed within analytical or automation platforms
- used to support consulting engagements
- incorporated into operational workflows

The procedure applies across all environments including development, testing, and production.

---

## 3. Lifecycle Principles

AI systems must be governed throughout their operational life.

Lifecycle governance ensures that:

- risks are assessed before deployment
- system behaviour is monitored after deployment
- significant changes are reviewed
- systems that are no longer appropriate are retired

Governance must remain proportionate to the potential impact of the system.

---

## 4. Lifecycle Stages

AI systems progress through defined lifecycle stages.

Each stage introduces specific governance activities.

---

### Stage 1. Concept and Initial Assessment

At this stage the purpose of the AI system is defined.

Key activities include:

- defining the system objective
- identifying intended users
- identifying data sources
- considering whether AI is appropriate for the problem

Initial governance questions should be addressed before development begins.

---

### Stage 2. Design and Architecture Review

The proposed system architecture is reviewed to ensure it meets organisational standards.

Key considerations include:

- system architecture
- data governance
- traceability and auditability
- operational reliability
- alignment with architectural principles

The Technical Architecture Lead may review the design before development proceeds.

---

### Stage 3. Risk Assessment

Before deployment the system must undergo risk assessment.

Risk assessment follows the AI Risk Management Framework.

The assessment considers potential risks including:

- regulatory exposure
- operational reliability
- misuse or unintended behaviour
- fairness and bias

Identified risks must be recorded in the AI Risk Register.

---

### Stage 4. Development and Testing

The system is implemented and tested.

Testing should ensure that:

- system behaviour matches the intended design
- outputs are appropriate for the intended use
- monitoring and logging capabilities are operational
- human oversight arrangements are functioning

Testing results should be documented where appropriate.

---

### Stage 5. Deployment Approval

Before deployment the system must receive approval from the relevant governance roles.

Deployment approval confirms that:

- risks have been assessed
- mitigation measures are in place
- monitoring arrangements exist
- human oversight is defined

Deployment may proceed once approval is granted.

---

### Stage 6. Operational Monitoring

After deployment the system must be monitored to ensure that it continues to operate as intended.

Monitoring activities may include:

- reviewing system outputs
- identifying unexpected behaviour
- detecting model drift or performance degradation
- reviewing audit logs

Monitoring may trigger additional review if unexpected behaviour is observed.

---

### Stage 7. Review and Retirement

AI systems must be reviewed periodically to determine whether they remain appropriate.

Systems may be retired where:

- they are no longer required
- the operational context has changed
- the system no longer performs adequately
- risks cannot be adequately mitigated

Retirement decisions should be documented.

---

## 5. Lifecycle Governance Checkpoints

Lifecycle stages introduce governance checkpoints.

These checkpoints ensure that systems do not progress without appropriate oversight.

Typical checkpoints include:

- architecture review
- risk assessment review
- deployment approval
- operational review

These checkpoints ensure that governance decisions are documented and traceable.

---

## 6. Documentation and Records

Lifecycle activities must be documented to provide evidence that governance procedures have been followed.

Documentation may include:

- system descriptions
- architectural review notes
- risk assessments
- deployment approval records
- monitoring reports

These records form part of the Artificial Intelligence Management System documentation.

---

## 7. Relationship to Other Governance Processes

This lifecycle procedure operates alongside other governance processes including:

- the AI Risk Management Framework
- the AI Risk Register
- the AI System Inventory
- the AI Incident Management Procedure

Together these processes ensure that AI systems are governed consistently across the organisation.

---

Document Owner: AI Governance Lead  
Review Cycle: Annual  
Approval Authority: Senior Management
