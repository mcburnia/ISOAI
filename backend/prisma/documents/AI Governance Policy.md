# Artificial Intelligence Governance Policy

## 1. Purpose

This policy establishes the governance principles that guide the design, development, deployment, and oversight of artificial intelligence systems within the organisation.

Artificial intelligence introduces new forms of operational capability and decision automation. It also introduces new categories of risk. These include regulatory exposure, unsafe automated decisions, opaque reasoning, and inappropriate reliance on automated outputs.

The purpose of this policy is to ensure that AI systems operated or advised upon by the organisation are governed in a controlled, auditable, and professionally responsible manner.

This policy forms the foundation of the organisation’s Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) established in alignment with ISO/IEC 42001.

---

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to all artificial intelligence systems that are:

- designed or developed by the organisation
- deployed within internal systems or advisory platforms
- evaluated or governed as part of consulting engagements
- used in research, analysis, or automated workflows

For the purposes of this policy, AI systems include:

- machine learning models
- large language model applications
- automated decision systems
- agent-based software automation
- predictive or classification models
- AI-assisted analytical tools

The policy applies to all environments including research, development, testing, and production.

---

## 3. Governance Principles

AI systems operated or advised upon by the organisation must follow the principles below.

### Accountability

AI systems must operate within clear lines of responsibility. Each system must have an identified owner responsible for its design, operation, and oversight.

Human accountability cannot be delegated to automated systems.

---

### Transparency and Explainability

Where AI systems influence analysis, recommendations, or operational decisions, the reasoning behind those outcomes must be capable of explanation.

Systems must retain sufficient evidence to reconstruct how decisions were produced, including model versions, input data sources, and decision logic where applicable.

---

### Human Oversight

Automated systems must operate under defined human oversight arrangements.

Where decisions may materially affect customers, regulatory obligations, or organisational risk exposure, a qualified human reviewer must retain authority to review, challenge, or override automated outcomes.

---

### Data Governance

AI systems must operate on governed data sources.

Data used for training, inference, or decision support must be:

- classified
- traceable to its origin
- subject to defined retention and access controls

Data lineage must be available where required for audit or regulatory review.

---

### Risk Awareness

AI systems must be assessed for potential risks before deployment.

Risk assessment must consider:

- regulatory impact
- operational safety
- potential bias or unfair outcomes
- misuse or unintended behaviour
- dependency on automated outputs

Risk mitigation measures must be documented and reviewed periodically.

---

### Auditability

AI activity must produce sufficient records to allow retrospective review.

These records may include:

- decision logs
- model version history
- input data references
- operational audit trails
- evidence of human intervention where applicable

Audit records must be retained in accordance with organisational retention policies.

---

### Responsible Use

AI systems must be used to assist professional judgement rather than replace it.

Automated outputs must be treated as analytical inputs rather than unquestioned authority.

Users must understand the limitations of automated reasoning and exercise appropriate professional judgement when interpreting outputs.

---

## 4. AI Management System

The organisation maintains a structured Artificial Intelligence Management System that governs the lifecycle of AI systems across the organisation.

The management system defines processes for:

- AI risk management
- system lifecycle governance
- operational monitoring
- incident response
- human oversight
- internal review and improvement

Supporting procedures and standards define how these activities are implemented in practice.

---

## 5. Roles and Responsibilities

Responsibility for AI governance is distributed across defined organisational roles.

Senior leadership retains ultimate accountability for the responsible operation of AI systems within the organisation.

Specific responsibilities include:

- oversight of AI governance policies
- review of significant AI risks
- approval of high-impact AI deployments
- monitoring of incidents and performance

Operational responsibilities for system design, implementation, and monitoring are defined in the AI Roles and Responsibilities framework.

---

## 6. Continuous Improvement

The AI Management System will be reviewed periodically to ensure it remains aligned with:

- evolving regulatory expectations
- advances in AI technology
- organisational experience operating automated systems
- lessons identified from incidents or operational review

Improvement actions arising from internal review or audit will be tracked and addressed through the organisation’s governance processes.

---

## 7. Policy Ownership

Policy Owner: The organisation Leadership  
Review Cycle: Annual  
Approval Authority: Senior Management

---

## 8. Effective Date

This policy takes effect immediately upon approval and applies to all AI-related activities undertaken by the organisation.
