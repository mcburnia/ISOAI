# AI Risk Management Framework

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the framework used by Gibbs Consulting to identify, assess, manage, and monitor risks associated with artificial intelligence systems.

Artificial intelligence systems introduce new operational and regulatory risks that must be assessed before systems are deployed and monitored throughout their lifecycle.

The purpose of this framework is to ensure that AI risks are evaluated consistently and managed in a controlled and auditable manner.

This framework forms part of the Artificial Intelligence Management System established in alignment with ISO/IEC 42001.

---

## 2. Scope

This framework applies to all artificial intelligence systems that fall within the scope of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

These include systems that are:

- designed or developed internally
- deployed as part of consulting engagements
- used to support analytical or advisory work
- incorporated into automated workflows or decision support tools

Risk assessment must be performed before deployment of an AI system and reviewed periodically during its operational life.

---

## 3. Principles of AI Risk Management

AI risk management within Gibbs Consulting follows several core principles.

### Proportionality

Risk assessment should be proportionate to the potential impact of the system.

Low impact analytical tools may require lighter assessment than systems capable of influencing operational or regulatory decisions.

---

### Transparency

Risk assessment decisions must be documented so that the reasoning behind risk classification can be reviewed.

This ensures that governance decisions remain transparent and defensible.

---

### Lifecycle Governance

Risk management is not a one-time activity. Risks must be reviewed periodically as systems evolve and operational conditions change.

Changes in model behaviour, data sources, or deployment context may introduce new risks.

---

### Human Accountability

Automated systems cannot assume responsibility for decisions.

Human oversight must remain central to governance of AI systems, particularly where outputs may influence professional judgement or operational decisions.

---

## 4. AI Risk Categories

AI systems may introduce risks across several domains.

The following categories provide a structured way to assess those risks.

### Regulatory Risk

The possibility that an AI system may produce outcomes that conflict with regulatory obligations or professional responsibilities.

Examples include:

- misuse of regulated data
- inappropriate automated decision making
- insufficient transparency for regulatory review

---

### Operational Risk

The possibility that system behaviour may produce incorrect or unreliable outputs that affect operational processes.

Examples include:

- model drift
- incorrect predictions or classifications
- reliance on incomplete data

---

### Data Risk

Risks arising from the quality, provenance, or governance of data used by the system.

Examples include:

- poor data quality
- incomplete data lineage
- use of data outside authorised purposes

---

### Bias and Fairness Risk

The possibility that automated outputs may systematically disadvantage particular individuals or groups.

Examples include:

- biased training data
- unbalanced datasets
- inappropriate model assumptions

---

### Security Risk

Risks associated with misuse or manipulation of AI systems.

Examples include:

- prompt injection
- adversarial inputs
- unauthorised model access

---

### Misuse Risk

The possibility that systems may be used in ways that were not originally intended.

Examples include:

- reliance on automated outputs without review
- application of models outside their design scope
- excessive automation of professional judgement

---

## 5. Risk Assessment Process

AI risks are assessed using a structured process.

The process typically includes the following stages.

### Step 1. System Description

The purpose, scope, and operational context of the AI system are documented.

This includes:

- system objectives
- input data sources
- expected outputs
- intended users

---

### Step 2. Risk Identification

Potential risks across the defined risk categories are identified.

This step considers how the system may fail, produce misleading outputs, or be used inappropriately.

---

### Step 3. Risk Evaluation

Identified risks are evaluated according to two dimensions.

Likelihood  
The probability that the risk may occur.

Impact  
The potential consequences if the risk occurs.

These dimensions provide an overall risk rating.

---

### Step 4. Risk Mitigation

Where risks exceed acceptable levels, mitigation measures must be defined.

Mitigation measures may include:

- improved data validation
- human review requirements
- additional monitoring
- technical controls within system architecture

---

### Step 5. Risk Documentation

All identified risks and mitigation measures must be recorded in the organisational AI Risk Register.

This provides visibility over AI risks across the organisation.

---

## 6. Risk Review

AI risks must be reviewed periodically.

Reviews may be triggered by:

- significant system changes
- changes in operational context
- incidents or unexpected system behaviour
- periodic governance review

Risk reviews ensure that mitigation measures remain appropriate.

---

## 7. Integration with AI Lifecycle Governance

Risk assessment forms part of the governance checkpoints within the AI system lifecycle.

Risk assessment must be completed before:

- deployment of new AI systems
- major changes to existing systems
- expansion of system scope or usage

Where risks are considered significant, deployment approval may require additional governance review.

---

## 8. Documentation and Records

Risk assessments must be documented to provide evidence that governance processes have been followed.

Documentation may include:

- system descriptions
- identified risks
- risk ratings
- mitigation actions
- review decisions

These records are retained as part of the Artificial Intelligence Management System.

---

## 9. Ownership and Review

Document Owner: AI Governance Lead  
Review Cycle: Annual  
Approval Authority: Senior Management
